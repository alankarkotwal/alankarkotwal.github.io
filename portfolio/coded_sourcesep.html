Optimizing Sensing Matrices for Compressed Sampling Recovery


Guide: Prof. Ajit Rajwade, CSE, IIT Bombay


Traditional approaches to visual compressed sensing have focused on single image sensing, with compression across space, as opposed to video sensing with compression across time. More recent work tries to achieve the latter with a learned 3–D dictionary, like the one below:



However, this approach assumes smoothness of pixel values in video frames across time and a fixed frame–rate. We attempt to solve two problems: relaxing this assumption with a source–separation approach in the same hardware framework and optimizing sensing matrix required for the source–separation approach to potentially achieve reconstructions better than those achievable with random matrices.

Our approach to solving the first problem is to model each image separately in the DCT domain, which gives a source-separation flavor to the problem. Traditional methods of solving the compressed sensing problem like the popular basis pursuit relaxation yield good results for reconstruction when we use Gaussian random measurement codes, but fail when we use the more realizable positive sensing codes.








Guarantees on the success of source separation involve the mutual coherence of the complete dictionary, that is, the product of the sensing matrices and the separate DCT dictionaries. To solve the second problem, we perform an explicit gradient descent on the coherence of the effective dictionary thus constructed. Further, since this optimization becomes prohibitive with increasing image size, we design matrices that have low mutual coherence in all their circular permutations and therefore can be tiled for overlapping patchwise reconstruction. We currently aim to design general sensing matrices with quantities that yield tighter error bounds than coherence. We thus explore bounds (like the l1–CSMV and l∞–based bounds) which ‘soften’ the sparsity criterion for l1 recovery, resulting in quantities verifiable in polynomial time, as opposed to the RIC bound which is combinatorial and therefore difficult to optimize.
