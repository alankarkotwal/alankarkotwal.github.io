Super-Resolution with Fourier Ptychographic Microscopy


Guide: Ashutosh Ricchariya, Ophthalmic Biophysics, L. V. Prasad Eye Institute, Hyderabad


The maximum number of independent samples that an imaging setup can extract from the imaged scene is dictated by the limits set by Fourier optics: if the imaged scene is wide–field, there are limits on how much one can zoom in. However, wide–field, high–resolution images are generally desirable in microscopy, pathology and eye imaging.




The state of the art in acquiring wide-field, high-resolution images is called ptychographic microscopy, where the imaging setup is zoomed in and images are taken by physically shifting the setup around. The acquired images are stitched in the spatial domain to get the final image. However, this involves complex and precise mechanical control.

The way to overcome this is to somehow shift the high-frequency information to low frequencies, where the system’s optics will not filter it. Then we image the resulting object, and computationally form the final high-resolution image by ‘stitching together’ the image in Fourier space using a phase retrieval algorithm. The advantage of this method is that we can perform this at low magnifications, still recover very high frequency information and hence acquire the ability to zoom in and see features to good resolution, resulting in a wide-field, high-resolution image.
